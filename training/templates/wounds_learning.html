Wound Care

Why This Matters

Wounds are common injuries that can happen in everyday situations. Proper wound care prevents infection and promotes healing. Learning these skills will help you confidently handle injuries and potentially prevent serious complications.

1
When to Seek Medical Attention
Uncontrolled Bleeding
Bleeding that doesn't stop after 10-15 minutes of direct pressure requires immediate medical attention.
Deep or Large Wounds
If a wound is deeper than 1/4 inch or exposes fat or muscle, stitches may be needed.
Location Concerns
Wounds on the face, hands, feet, genitals, or over major joints should be evaluated due to functional and cosmetic risks.
Facial wounds may leave visible scars and should be treated by a professional.
Foreign Objects
If debris remains embedded after cleaning, medical help is needed. Never attempt to remove deeply embedded objects yourself.
Animal or Human Bites
High risk of infection; often requires antibiotics. All bites that break the skin should be seen by a doctor.
Animal bites can transmit rabies and other serious infections.
Tetanus Risk
Wounds from dirty or rusty objects need attention, especially if vaccinations are outdated. Tetanus boosters are typically needed every 10 years.
2
First Aid Treatment at Home
1
Wash Your Hands

Prevent infection by cleaning your hands thoroughly before touching the wound.

Use soap and water or hand sanitizer with at least 60% alcohol.
2
Stop the Bleeding

Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth or bandage. Elevate the wound above heart level if possible.

3
Clean the Wound

Rinse under running water to remove dirt. Avoid soap directly in the wound as it can irritate tissue.

4
Apply Antibiotic Ointment

Helps prevent infection and keeps the wound moist, which promotes healing.

5
Protect the Wound

Cover with a sterile bandage, changing it daily or when dirty. This keeps the wound clean and and protects it from dirt and bacteria which may cause infection.

3
Recognizing Infection

Even with proper care, infections may develop. Watch for these warning signs:

Increased Redness & Swelling

Spreading redness or swelling around the wound, especially with warmth.

Pus or Discharge

Yellow or green fluid oozing from the wound indicates infection.

Fever

A temperature above 100.4°F (38°C) may indicate an infection.

Persistent Pain

Pain that worsens or does not improve over time.

If any of these symptoms occur, seek medical attention to prevent serious complications.
