Fractures and Sprains

Why This Matters

Fractures and sprains are common injuries that can happen during sports, falls, or accidents. Knowing how to identify and provide initial care for these injuries can prevent further damage and speed up recovery. Let's learn how to handle these injuries with confidence!

1
Understanding Fractures and Sprains
Fracture

A break in the bone, ranging from a small crack to a complete break. It occurs due to excessive force, falls, or direct impacts.

Symptoms:
  • • Sharp pain that worsens with movement
  • • Visible deformity in severe cases
  • • Grating sensation or inability to move the limb
  • • Swelling and bruising
Sprain

The stretching or tearing of ligaments—the tough bands connecting bones at a joint. Sprains happen when a joint is twisted or overstretched.

Symptoms:
  • • Aching discomfort that worsens with joint use
  • • Swelling may be significant
  • • Joint retains some function
  • • May still allow partial movement despite pain
Difficult to Distinguish

Both injuries cause pain, swelling, and bruising, making them difficult to distinguish at first. When in doubt, treat the injury as a fracture and seek medical attention.

2
How to Splint a Fracture
1
Keep the Person Still

Do not attempt to realign the bone. This could cause further damage.

Never try to push a protruding bone back in or straighten a deformed limb.
2
Find a Firm Object

Use a padded board, rolled towel, or magazine as a splint. The splint should extend beyond the joints above and below the fracture.

3
Position the Splint

Place it alongside the injured limb, ensuring it supports the joints above and below the fracture.

4
Secure the Splint

Use bandages or cloth strips, making sure they are snug but not overly tight. You should be able to slip a finger between the bandage and skin.

5
Monitor for Complications

Regularly check for swelling, discoloration, or numbness, which may indicate restricted blood flow.

How to Wrap a Sprain

Wrapping provides support, reduces swelling, and prevents further injury. Follow these steps:

1
Position the Limb

Keep the limb comfortably and slightly elevated. Make sure the person is in a stable, comfortable position.

2
Start Wrapping

Begin below the injured joint, ensuring the joint is slightly flexed, and continue upward in a figure-eight pattern, overlapping the bandage by about half its width.

3
Apply Proper Pressure

Maintain firm but gentle pressure—tight enough for support, but not so tight as to cut off circulation.

4
Monitor and Adjust

Adjust the wrap if swelling increases or if numbness occurs. Check circulation frequently.

4
RICE Protocol for sprains
1
R: Rest

Avoid using the injured joint in order to prevent further aggravating the sprain.

2
I: Ice

Apply Ice for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours for 1-2 days

3
C: Compress

Wrap the joint in the manner explained in the previous section to stabilise it and prevent further injury. This also limits swelling

4
E: Elevate

Keep the injured joint above heart level to encourage fluid drainage and reduce swelling in the injured joint.
