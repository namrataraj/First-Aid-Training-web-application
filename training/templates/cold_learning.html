Cold Exposure First Aid

Why This Matters

Exposure to extreme cold can lead to serious health issues such as hypothermia and frostbite. These conditions can develop quickly and may become life-threatening if not addressed promptly. Recognizing the symptoms early and knowing how to respond can prevent severe complications or even death. This module will teach you essential skills for handling cold exposure emergencies.

1
Hypothermia

Hypothermia occurs when the body temperature drops below 95°F (35°C), causing the body to lose heat faster than it can produce it.

Symptoms of Hypothermia
  • • Shivering (although shivering may stop in severe cases)
  • • Slurred speech or mumbling
  • • Confusion or disorientation
  • • Numbness, fatigue, or weakness
  • • Numbness, fatigue, or weakness
  • • Loss of coordination or clumsiness
  • • Slow or irregular heartbeat and breathing
  • • Loss of consciousness (severe cases)
Treatment for Hypothermia
  • 1. Move the person to a warm, dry location or a heated building or vehicle
  • 2. Remove wet clothing and replace it with dry, warm layers
  • 3. Warm the person gradually by wrapping them in blankets, using warm dry towels, or applying warm water bottles to their armpits, chest, or groin area
  • 4. Provide warm non-alcoholic and non-caffeinated drinks. Avoid giving alcohol as it can lower body temperature
  • 5. Monitor their condition and seek medical help if the symptoms persist or worsen
Important Warning

If the person is unconscious or unresponsive, call emergency services immediately. Do not attempt to warm them too quickly, as this can cause complications such as cardiac arrhythmias.

2
Frostbite

Frostbite occurs when body tissues freeze due to prolonged exposure to cold, particularly in extremities like fingers, toes, ears, and nose.

Symptoms of Frostbite
  • • Skin that appears white, gray, yellowish, or waxy
  • • Numbness or a "pins and needles" sensation
  • • Hard or stiff skin that's difficult to move
  • • Blisters (in severe cases)
  • • The area may feel cold to the touch
Treatment for Frostbite
  • 1. Get the person to a warmer environment immediately
  • 2. Remove any jewelry and place the affected area in warm water (100°F-104°F / 37°C-40°C). Do not use direct heat like a heating pad or fire, as it may cause further damage
  • 3. Rewarm the affected area slowly and gently. Do not rub or massage frostbitten skin, as this can cause further injury
  • 4. Protect the affected areas by wrapping them in sterile dressings or gauze
  • 5. Seek medical attention immediately for severe frostbite. Rewarming should only be done if the person is in a safe, warm environment
Important Note

Never rub snow on frostbitten areas or use dry heat. This can cause more damage to the already injured tissue. Also, if there's any chance the area might refreeze, it's better not to thaw it at all, as refreezing can cause even more severe tissue damage.

3
Finding Shelter and Preventing Cold Exposure

Preventing cold exposure is always better than treating it. Here are strategies for finding shelter and staying warm:

1. Find Shelter
  • If possible, take shelter in a building, vehicle, or any structure that can block the wind and provide warmth
  • In wilderness situations, look for natural shelters like caves, dense tree cover, or areas protected from wind
  • If necessary, create an emergency shelter using tarps, branches, or snow (snow caves can provide insulation)
2. Insulate Your Body
  • Insulate your body with dry clothing and materials such as wool or synthetic fabrics that retain heat. Avoid wearing cotton, as it can trap moisture and cause further heat loss
  • Layer clothing: use a moisture-wicking base layer, insulating middle layers, and a waterproof/windproof outer layer
  • Don't forget to cover your head, hands, and feet, as these areas lose heat quickly
  • If clothing gets wet, change into dry clothes as soon as possible
3. Avoid Alcohol

While it might make you feel warm, alcohol actually lowers your core body temperature and increases the risk of cold exposure. It dilates blood vessels near the skin, which can cause heat to escape more quickly from your body.

4. Keep Moving

If possible, continue moving to generate body heat. However, be careful not to overexert yourself, as it can lead to exhaustion and increased heat loss through sweating. If you're stranded, conserve energy by moving just enough to stay warm.

5. Stay Hydrated

Drink warm non-caffeinated and, non-alcoholic beverages to maintain hydration. Dehydration can worsen cold exposure and impair your body's ability to regulate temperature.
