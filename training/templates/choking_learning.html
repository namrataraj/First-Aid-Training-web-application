Choking First Aid

Why This Matters

Choking occurs when an object blocks the airway, preventing normal breathing. If not treated immediately, choking can lead to suffocation and death within minutes. Knowing how to recognize and respond to choking can save a life. These skills are essential for everyone, especially parents, caregivers, and those who work with children.

1
Recognizing Choking
Unable to Speak, Cough, or Breathe

The person cannot talk, make noise, or breathe effectively. This is the most serious sign.

Universal Choking Sign

The person clutches their throat with one or both hands. This is a universal distress signal for choking.

Blue-Tinted Lips or Face

Cyanosis (blue coloration) occurs due to lack of oxygen and indicates a severe emergency.

Unusual Sounds

High-pitched wheezing sounds while inhaling or complete silence can indicate choking.

Important Distinction

If the person is coughing forcefully, encourage them to keep coughing. This indicates their airway is not completely blocked, and they may be able to clear the obstruction themselves. Only intervene if they cannot cough, speak, or breathe.

2
The Heimlich Maneuver

The Heimlich maneuver is a lifesaving technique used to dislodge an obstruction from the airway. It varies based on the person's age.

Watch Heimlich Demonstration
3
For Adults
1
Stand Behind the Person

Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist.

2
Make a Fist

Make a fist with one hand and place it just above their belly button (navel).

3
Grab Your Fist

Grab your fist with your other hand and perform quick, inward and upward thrusts.

4
Repeat Until Successful

Repeat until the object is expelled or the person becomes unresponsive.

4
For Children (Ages 1-8)

The technique for children is similar to adults but with some important modifications:

1
Same Technique, Less Force

Use the same technique as for adults but with reduced force to avoid injury.

2
Kneel If Necessary

Kneel behind the child if necessary to be at the appropriate height.

3
Gentler Abdominal Thrusts

Perform gentler abdominal thrusts using less force than on an adult.

4
Stop When Successful

Stop when the object is dislodged or if the child becomes unconscious.

5
For Infants (Under 1 Year Old)

The technique for infants is significantly different due to their small size and fragility:

1
Position the Infant

Lay the baby face-down along your forearm, supporting their head and neck. Keep their head lower than their body.

2
Back Blows

Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades using the heel of your hand.

3
Turn the Infant Over

If the object is not dislodged, turn the infant onto their back while supporting their head.

4
Chest Thrusts

Give 5 chest thrusts using two fingers, pushing down about 1.5 inches on the center of the chest.

5
Repeat the Cycle

Repeat the cycle of 5 back blows and 5 chest thrusts until the object is expelled or the infant becomes unresponsive.

Important Warning

Never perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver) on infants as this can cause internal injuries. Stick to the back blow and chest thrust technique.

6
For Yourself (Self-Heimlich)

If you're alone and choking, you can perform the Heimlich maneuver on yourself:

1
Make a Fist

Make a fist and place it above your belly button, below your ribcage.

2
Grab Your Fist

Grab your fist with your other hand and press inward and upward sharply.

3
Use a Sturdy Object

Alternatively, lean over a sturdy object like a chair back, counter, or railing, and push your abdomen forcefully against it.

4
Repeat Until Successful

Repeat until the object is expelled.

7
When to Seek Medical Help
Unconsciousness

If the person becomes unconscious at any point during the choking episode.

Persistent Choking

If choking persists despite multiple attempts at the Heimlich maneuver.

After Successful Removal

After successful removal of the object, especially in infants or children, as internal injury may have occurred.

Follow-Up Care

Even if the choking episode seems resolved, watch for signs of respiratory distress, persistent cough, difficulty swallowing, or chest/throat pain, which could indicate complications requiring medical attention.
