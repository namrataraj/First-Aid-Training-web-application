Heat-Related First Aid

Why This Matters

Exposure to extreme heat can lead to serious health conditions, including heat exhaustion and heatstroke. These conditions can escalate quickly and become life-threatening if not properly addressed. Knowing how to recognize the symptoms and provide appropriate first aid can prevent serious complications and potentially save lives, especially during hot weather or in situations where people are exerting themselves in high temperatures.

1
Heat Exhaustion

Heat exhaustion is a condition that occurs when the body overheats. It's a milder form of heat-related illness that can develop after exposure to high temperatures and inadequate fluid replacement.

Symptoms
  • • Heavy sweating
  • • Weakness or dizziness
  • • Cool, clammy skin
  • • Nausea or vomiting
  • • Fast but weak pulse
  • • Muscle cramps and headache
Treatment
  • • Move to a cool, shaded area or indoors
  • • Loosen tight clothing and apply cool, damp cloths
  • • Encourage slow sipping of water or electrolyte-rich fluids
  • • Rest until symptoms improve
Important Warning

If symptoms persist for over an hour or worsen, seek medical help. Heat exhaustion can progress to heatstroke, which is a life-threatening emergency.

2
Heatstroke (Medical Emergency)

Heatstroke is a severe and potentially life-threatening condition that occurs when the body's temperature regulation system fails and body temperature rises to dangerous levels (above 104°F or 40°C).

Symptoms
  • • High body temperature (above 104°F / 40°C)
  • • Hot, dry skin (no sweating)
  • • Confusion or slurred speech
  • • Rapid, strong pulse
  • • Loss of consciousness
Treatment
  • • Call emergency services immediately! Heatstroke can be fatal.
  • • Move the person to shade and remove excess clothing
  • • Cool the body rapidly using water, ice packs, or fanning
  • • If conscious, offer small sips of cool water
  • • Avoid caffeine or alcohol, which can worsen dehydration
Critical Emergency

Heatstroke is a medical emergency that requires immediate professional attention. Every minute counts. While waiting for emergency services, focus on cooling the person down as quickly as possible.

3
Finding Help & Improvising in a Remote Location

If you're in a remote location and someone is suffering from heat-related illness, you may need to improvise while seeking help. Here are some strategies:

1. Locate Shade & Shelter
  • Seek natural shade (trees, caves, or large rocks)
  • Improvise a sun shelter with clothing, branches, or emergency blankets
2. Hydration & Water Sources
  • Conserve water, not energy—rest in the shade
  • Look for dew, rivers, or underground water (dig in dry riverbeds)
  • If needed, filter and purify water using boiling, filtration, or solar disinfection
3. Cooling Strategies
  • Use evaporation: Wet clothes with any available water and let the breeze cool the body
  • Bury water bottles partially in sand to keep them cooler
  • Rest during the hottest hours (midday) and move in the early morning or evening
4. Signaling for Help
  • Use a mirror, bright clothing, or a fire to signal rescuers
  • Create large symbols (SOS) on the ground using rocks or branches
  • If near a road, leave signs (arrows, footprints) pointing to your location
